Designing Data-Intensive Applications

The Big Ideas Behind Reliable, Scalable, and Maintainable Systems

By Martin Kleppmann

About the Book

Data is central to many system design challenges today, including scalability, consistency, reliability, efficiency, and maintainability . This book helps navigate the diverse landscape of technologies for processing and storing data by examining the pros and cons of various tools like relational databases, NoSQL datastores, stream/batch processors, and message brokers . It focuses on the fundamental principles that remain constant even as software changes, enabling software engineers and architects to apply these ideas effectively in modern applications .

The book aims to help you make informed decisions about system architecture, understand trade-offs, learn from major online services, and grasp the underlying distributed systems research . It delves into the internals of data systems, their key algorithms, principles, and trade-offs, providing intuition for their behavior and enabling better design decisions .

Who Should Read This Book?

This book is for software engineers, software architects, and technical managers involved in developing applications with server-side data storage or processing . It's particularly relevant if you make architectural decisions, choose tools, or want a deeper understanding of the systems you use . Some familiarity with web applications, relational databases, and SQL is assumed .

This book is valuable if you want to:

  • Learn how to build scalable, highly available, and robust data systems .
  • Make systems easier to maintain long-term .
  • Understand the internals of major online services and database technologies .

Key Concepts Covered

The book focuses on three core concerns for data systems :

  • Reliability: Systems should work correctly even in the face of hardware faults, software errors, and human mistakes . Topics include fault tolerance and resilience .
  • Scalability: Systems should have reasonable ways to deal with growth in data volume, traffic, or complexity . This involves describing load and performance, including concepts like latency percentiles and throughput .
  • Maintainability: Systems should be easy for different people to work on productively over time . This includes operability, simplicity (managing complexity), and evolvability (ease of making changes) .
